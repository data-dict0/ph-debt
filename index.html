Understanding the Philippine government debt

When it comes to government affairs, debt is a hated word and is almost synonymous to bad news.

But few people outside the bureaucracy really understand it and what it means in public finance.

Every month, the Philippine government reports its level of outstanding debt. As of August, that amount stood at P14.35 trillion, according to the Bureau of the Treasury.

The absolute value looks astonishing. But there are many things that go behind and beyond this number, so let's examine it.


The amount of debt reported monthly only covers liabilities owed by the national government. It does not include obligations incurred by cities, municipalities and provinces or some government financial institutions like the Social Security System or the Philippine Health Insurance Corp. (PhilHealth). These are reported separately.
Next, debt is owed either in pesos (domestic) or in other currencies (external or foreign). The government has historically borrowed mainly in pesos or from local sources because doing so has benefits like protection against foreign exchange swings that can increase the value of debt.
As of August 2023, the latest period for which data is available, 68% of national government debt were domestically sourced, while the balance of 32% were in various currencies like the US dollar, euro, yen or sterling.

While trillions of pesos in debt appears alarming, policymakers have avoided raising red flags on account of the absolute value of debt. Instead, they closely watch a different metric altogether, one that economists believe better measure how sustainable the government’s debt pile is, or in simple words, how capable are we in settling our debt on time.
In economics, this is the debt to GDP ratio or the proportion of the debt to the economy's gross domestic product— the sum of all products and services created in an economy. The idea behind the debt ratio is, so long as the economy grows faster than the government's debt, enough revenues are generated to cover your obligations. After all, governments mainly incur debt when they have to bridge the gap between what they earn as revenues and what they spend on.
On this front, the Philippines has some ground to recover. As of the first quarter, debt accounted for 61% of GDP, Treasury data showed, far higher than the record-low of 39.6% recorded in 2019.

Debt piled up when the pandemic struck because the Duterte administration had to borrow more than it initially projected to fund its response to the health crisis. Programs such as cash support to laid off workers and poor households as well as vaccine procurement added to typical public projects every year, pushing up spending costs the past three years.
What corresponds to a healthy debt to GDP differs, although not widely and typically ranges from 50-60%. That said, Finance Secretary Benjamin Diokno, who also served as central bank government under the Duterte government, was not worried.
“It went up because we had to borrow money for medicines, plus revenues went down…As a result of the pandemic, it's reasonable,” he said in a briefing last July.

The lenders
Apart from the debt level, interest is also monitored. Like borrowing from a bank, interest make a debt more expensive. While the government has little to no control on how much interest lenders charge, it does have the ability to influence it. This is where things like credit ratings matter because they serve as a barometer of the government's ability to pay, potentially inducing creditors to lend at a lower interest.
Speaking of lenders, many think that we borrow a lot through loans from institutions like the World Bank or other countries like Japan and the U.S. While we do count them as among our creditors, our biggest lenders are in fact local investors.

These may cover banks, private companies to insurance firms which have funds to park and grow for future needs of their clients. It's not very straightforward, but just imagine how your life insurance pledges to grow your money after 10 or 20 years after. These also include just another person wanting to invest or so-called retail investors. All of them lend the government by buying Treasury bonds or bills.
The government “sells” T-bonds or T-bills in the local market— or borrows— every week, and sells the same periodically to foreign lenders. These papers accounted for over 85% of the debt pile as of August, data showed. The problem the government now faces now is compared to nearly a decade ago, a huge chunk of this type of debt borrowed by selling T-bonds are falling due during the Marcos administration.
