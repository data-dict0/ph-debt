Understanding the Philippine government debt

When it comes to government affairs, debt is a hated word and is almost synonymous to bad news.

But few people outside the bureaucracy really understand it and what it means in public finance.

Every month, the Philippine government reports its level of outstanding debt. As of August, that amount stood at P14.35 trillion, according to the Bureau of the Treasury.

The absolute value looks astonishing. But there are many things that go behind and beyond this number, so let's examine it.


First thing to know is that the amount only covers debt owed by the national government. It does not include liabilities incurred by cities, municipalities and provinces or some government financial institutions like the Social Security System or the Philippine Health Insurance Corp. (PhilHealth). These are reported separately.
Next, debt is owed either in pesos (domestic) or in other currencies (external or foreign). The government has historically borrowed mainly in pesos or from local sources because doing so has benefits like protection against foreign exchange swings that can increase the value of foreign obligations. As of August 2023, 63% of national government debt were domestically sourced, while the balance of 37% were in various currencies like the US dollar, euro, yen or sterling.

While the government records the amount it borrows every month, it closely watches a different metric altogether to ensure the sustainability of its debt pile, or in simple words, to ensure we can pay our obligations on time. This is because you cannot really avoid debt, Nicholas Antonio Mapa, senior economist at ING Bank in Manila, said.
Policymakers instead monitor the proportion of the government’s debt over gross domestic product (GDP), or the sum of all products and services created in an economy. The idea is, so long as you grow your economy faster than your debt, it means you are using funds you borrowed productively which in turn, ensures that you generate enough resources to meet your obligations.

On this front, the Philippines has some ground to recover. As of the first quarter, debt accounted for 61% of GDP, Treasury data showed. The ratio hit a record-low of 39.6% in 2019, but when the pandemic struck, the Duterte administration had to borrow more than it initially projected to fund its response to the health crisis including cash support to laid off workers and poor households and vaccine procurement, among others. Finance Secretary Benjamin Diokno, who also served as central bank government under the Duterte government, was not worried.
“It went up because we had to borrow money for medicines, plus revenues went down. But the last number in the first quarter was about 61%. As a result of the pandemic, it's reasonable,” Diokno said in a briefing last July.
Apart from the debt level, interest is also monitored. Like borrowing from a bank, interest make a debt more expensive. While the government has little to no control on how much interest lenders charge, it does have the ability to influence it. This is where things like credit ratings may matter because they provide a barometer on how good of a borrower the government is, and therefore may induce lenders to lend at a lower interest.
